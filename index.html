Clean energy sources are types of energy that do not release harmful emissions or pollutants, such as greenhouse gases, sulfur dioxide, or nitrogen oxides. These types of energy are also referred to as renewable energy, as they are naturally replenished and don't use up natural resources. Examples of clean energy sources include solar, wind, hydro, geothermal, and biomass energy.

As the impact of climate change becomes increasingly concerning, the adoption of clean energy has become crucial to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) recommends that a shift towards renewable energy sources is necessary to limit global warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels and prevent the most severe impacts of climate change. Therefore, transitioning to clean energy is necessary to protect the environment and the well-being of future generations.

As a result of climate change, we are already seeing significant negative consequences for the environment. Nowadays, glaciers are melting at an accelerated rate, for example in Montana's Glacier National Park, the number of glaciers in 1910 was 150, and today the number of glaciers has decreased to 30.

Over the last hundred years, the concentration of carbon dioxide (CO2) has increased by 40%, which is the highest level in the last 650,000 years.

Melting ice is causing sea level to rise, with the rise occurring at a much faster rate in recent years (3.2 millimeters per year). As a result of melting ice, a large number of fauna is displaced or disappears; for example, the population of Adélie penguins in Antarctica has decreased by 90%.

However, the global average surface temperature has increased by almost 2 degrees Fahrenheit (1.1 degrees Celsius) since 1750. This is in turn affecting ecosystems and communities all across the world, with some regions experiencing severe drought or heavy rains, earthquakes, floods, frequent fires or lack of drinking water; a large number of fauna and flora are disappearing or migrating.

But the future progression of these effects depends on the total amount of carbon dioxide and other Greenhouse Gases emitted and the extent to which the global ecosystem is allowed to irreversibly worsen before taking drastic action. If we cut emissions soon, we can avoid some of the worst consequences now before they become irreversible aspects of a worsening global climate.

Thus, the sea level has already risen by 0.2 meters since 1880, and scientists predict that the sea level may rise 0,3 meters by 2100 or 2,4 meters with carbon emissions at the current level.

Between 20 and 31 million people in the United States live in areas that will be underwater unless significant reductions in greenhouse gas emissions occur. Scientists have come to this conclusion, reports "Voice of America" with reference to Reuters.

In addition, humanity will be left without a significant supply of fresh water as a result of melting glaciers, that stores about 3/4 of the world's supply of fresh water.

As a result of the rising sea levels, low-elevation countries like the Netherlands, Denmark, large coastlines around India, China, and Australia, as well as many islands like the Maldives, Kiribati, and Jakarta will all experience flooding and permanent loss of land. This is only a primary effect of the rising sea-levels, not to mention further disasters that would be caused as flooding destroys coastal power systems, shipping infrastructure, and habitats.

Some diseases, such as malaria, will become more widespread. Additionally, as average global temperatures rise, the human body’s natural defenses may become less effective as fungi, bacteria, and viruses, kept at bay by our relatively high body temperature, will evolve to live at higher temperatures closer to our own internal body temperature, and thus be more capable of infecting humans, whose evolutionary adaptation cannot respond as quickly as will that of the microorganisms.

Scientists conclude that the intensity of hurricanes and the frequency of the most powerful hurricanes (Category 4 and 5) have increased significantly since the 1880s. According to scientists' forecasts, hurricanes will become stronger and more frequent, and the amount of precipitation will increase even more.

Scientists predict an increase in the intensity of droughts and abnormal heat waves and all seasons becoming hotter for most countries. Some countries are likely to enter the stage of mega-drought. A large part of the flora and fauna will disappear.

The number of forest fires will increase, even in rainy regions. For example, in the US, the cost of fighting forest fires has increased 11 times over the past 30 years.

Fuels such as soft coal and gas account for about 75% of greenhouse gas emissions and 90% of carbon dioxide emissions. These types of fuel are used as the main sources of energy all over the world. As greenhouse gases cover our planet, they catch the sun's rays and accumulate heat, thus causing climate change.

Some actions that can be taken to address the challenges related to this goal include:

Reduce energy consumption

We can reduce our energy consumption by adopting energy-efficient practices at home, such as turning off lights and unplugging electronics when not in use, using energy-efficient appliances, and adjusting the thermostat to conserve energy.

Use renewable energy sources

We can install solar panels or wind turbines on our property to generate our own renewable energy. If this is not feasible, we can consider purchasing renewable energy from a supplier that sources energy from renewable sources.

Support policies that promote clean energy

We can support policies and regulations that encourage the deployment of renewable energy, such as by advocating for tax incentives, feed-in tariffs, and renewable portfolio standards.

Reduce carbon footprint

We can reduce our carbon footprint by adopting sustainable transportation practices, such as carpooling, biking, or walking instead of driving alone.

Raise awareness

We can raise awareness about the importance of clean energy and its benefits by educating ourselves and others, sharing information on social media, and supporting organizations that promote clean energy solutions.

Countries that are paving the way for a sustainable future

Denmark is a world leader in wind energy, with over 40% of its electricity coming from wind power. The country has also set a target to be 100% reliant on renewable energy by 2050.

Sweden has set an ambitious goal of being carbon neutral by 2045, and the country is well on its way to achieving this target. It has already reduced its greenhouse gas emissions by 25% since 1990 and is increasing its use of renewable energy sources.

Costa Rica has a goal of becoming carbon neutral by 2050 and has made significant progress in increasing its use of renewable energy sources, particularly hydropower and geothermal.

Germany is a leader in solar power, with over 50% of the world's installed solar capacity. The country has also set a target of phasing out nuclear power and transitioning to a renewable energy-based economy.

Iceland has virtually eliminated the use of fossil fuels and generates nearly 100% of its electricity from renewable sources, primarily geothermal and hydropower. The country has also become a leader in the production of green hydrogen, a promising fuel source for the future.
